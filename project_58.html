Project 58: Reinforcement Learning for Pricing — Bandit Algorithms

Multi-Armed Bandit Epsilon-Greedy UCB Thompson Sampling Regret

Difficulty: Very Hard  |  Builds on: Projects 30, 52

1 · Hypothetical Problem

  • An e-commerce company sells a premium headphone product and is considering 5 price points: $49, $59, $69, $79, $89
  • Each price has an unknown purchase probability — lower prices convert more but generate less revenue per sale
  • The company wants to find the optimal price (maximizing expected revenue) as quickly as possible
  • Every customer shown a suboptimal price represents lost revenue — this is the exploration-exploitation tradeoff
  • Traditional A/B testing (Project 30) would split traffic equally across all 5 prices for weeks — too much wasted revenue
  • Goal → use bandit algorithms to adaptively allocate traffic, converging on the best price while minimizing cumulative regret

2 · How to Approach It

  1. From Project 30: A/B testing gives statistical rigor but wastes traffic on clearly inferior options
  2. From Project 52: deployed models need to make real-time decisions — bandits are online learning algorithms
  3. The multi-armed bandit problem: K "arms" (prices), each with unknown reward distribution; pull one arm per customer
  4. Exploration: try different prices to learn their conversion rates
  5. Exploitation: show the price you currently believe is best to maximize revenue
  6. The goal is to minimize cumulative regret — the total revenue lost compared to always showing the optimal price

3 · How Can We Solve It

  1. Define 5 price points with hidden purchase probabilities (the "true" conversion rates we're trying to learn)
  2. Implement epsilon-greedy: explore randomly with probability ε, exploit the best-known arm otherwise
  3. Implement UCB1 (Upper Confidence Bound): choose the arm with the highest optimistic estimate
  4. Implement Thompson Sampling: sample from posterior distributions and choose the arm with the highest sample
  5. Simulate 10,000 customers arriving one at a time
  6. Track cumulative regret, arm selection frequency, and estimated vs. true conversion rates
  7. Compare all three algorithms against random selection and the oracle (always optimal) baseline

4 · What Should Do

  • ✅ Define regret clearly — regret = (optimal reward) - (actual reward) per step; cumulative regret should grow sublinearly
  • ✅ Use decaying epsilon for epsilon-greedy — explore more early, exploit more later
  • ✅ Track confidence intervals — UCB and Thompson Sampling naturally provide uncertainty estimates
  • ✅ Run multiple simulations — bandit algorithms are stochastic; average over 100+ runs for reliable comparisons
  • ✅ Consider the revenue metric, not just conversion rate — a $89 price with 10% conversion beats $49 with 15% conversion
  • ✅ Use Thompson Sampling as the default — it's empirically the best general-purpose bandit algorithm

5 · What Should NOT Do

  • ❌ Do NOT use pure exploration (random) — you'll waste half your traffic on bad prices
  • ❌ Do NOT use pure exploitation (greedy) — you'll lock onto the first price that looks good and miss the true optimum
  • ❌ Do NOT set epsilon too high — ε=0.5 means 50% random exploration; ε=0.05–0.1 is typical
  • ❌ Do NOT ignore non-stationarity — if conversion rates change over time, use sliding window or discounted estimates
  • ❌ Do NOT compare algorithms on a single run — variance is high; use averaged regret curves
  • ❌ Do NOT forget the business context — a 1% regret reduction on 1M customers is $100K+ in revenue

6 · When to Use What

  • 🔹 Epsilon-Greedy → simplest to implement; good baseline; works well with decaying ε
  • 🔹 UCB1 → deterministic; good theoretical guarantees; explores systematically based on uncertainty
  • 🔹 Thompson Sampling → Bayesian; best empirical performance; naturally balances exploration/exploitation
  • 🔹 A/B Testing (Project 30) → when you need statistical significance and can afford fixed exploration
  • 🔹 Contextual Bandits → when the optimal arm depends on user features (personalized pricing)
  • 🔹 Full RL (Q-learning, Policy Gradient) → when actions affect future states (not just immediate rewards)

7 · When NOT to Use

  • 🚫 When you need strict statistical significance — bandits don't provide p-values like A/B tests
  • 🚫 When the action space is continuous — standard bandits need discrete arms; use Bayesian optimization instead
  • 🚫 When rewards are delayed by days/weeks — bandits assume immediate feedback
  • 🚫 When there are only 2 options — a simple A/B test is clearer and provides confidence intervals
  • 🚫 When the environment is adversarial — standard bandits assume stochastic rewards; use EXP3 for adversarial settings

8 · How It Only Works — Internal Mechanics

The Multi-Armed Bandit Problem

  • K arms (actions), each with an unknown reward distribution
  • At each time step t, the agent picks one arm and observes a reward
  • Goal: maximize cumulative reward over T rounds (equivalently, minimize cumulative regret)
  • Regret at time t: r_t = μ* - μ_{a_t} where μ* is the best arm's true mean
  • Cumulative regret R_T = Σ r_t — should grow sublinearly (O(√T) or O(log T)) for good algorithms

Epsilon-Greedy

  • With probability ε: pick a random arm (explore)
  • With probability 1-ε: pick the arm with the highest estimated reward (exploit)
  • Estimated reward Q(a) = (sum of rewards from arm a) / (number of times arm a was pulled)
  • Decaying ε: ε_t = ε₀ / t — explore less as you learn more
  • Regret: O(ε × T) for fixed ε — linear regret; O(log T) with proper decay

UCB1 (Upper Confidence Bound)

  • Pick the arm that maximizes: Q(a) + c × √(ln(t) / N(a))
  • Q(a) = estimated mean reward; N(a) = number of times arm a was pulled
  • The second term is the "exploration bonus" — large for rarely-pulled arms
  • Deterministic — no randomness in arm selection (unlike ε-greedy)
  • Regret: O(K × log T) — logarithmic, near-optimal

Thompson Sampling

  • Maintain a posterior distribution for each arm's reward (Beta distribution for binary rewards)
  • At each step: sample θ_a ~ Beta(α_a, β_a) for each arm; pick the arm with highest sample
  • Update: if reward=1, α_a += 1; if reward=0, β_a += 1 (Bayesian update from Project 30)
  • Naturally balances exploration and exploitation — uncertain arms get sampled more
  • Regret: O(K × log T) — matches UCB1 theoretically, often better empirically

Revenue vs. Conversion

  • Reward = price × conversion (not just conversion rate)
  • A $89 price with 10% conversion yields $8.90 expected revenue per visitor
  • A $49 price with 15% conversion yields $7.35 — lower despite higher conversion
  • The bandit optimizes expected revenue, not conversion rate

9 · Common Pitfalls and Failure Modes

  • ⚠️ Fixed epsilon — never decaying ε means you explore forever; 10% of traffic always wasted
  • ⚠️ Cold start — all algorithms need initial exploration; the first 50-100 customers see random prices
  • ⚠️ Non-stationarity — if conversion rates change over time (seasonality), standard bandits don't adapt
  • ⚠️ Delayed rewards — if you don't know if a customer purchased until days later, the feedback loop breaks
  • ⚠️ Evaluating on a single run — bandits are stochastic; one run can be misleading; average over 100+ simulations
  • ⚠️ Ignoring context — if different customer segments have different optimal prices, standard bandits miss this

10 · Scalability Considerations

  • All three algorithms are O(K) per step — trivial computation even with 100 arms
  • Thompson Sampling requires sampling from Beta distributions — microseconds per sample
  • UCB1 requires computing log and sqrt — negligible overhead
  • Memory: O(K) — just store counts and sum of rewards per arm
  • For contextual bandits (personalized pricing), complexity grows with feature dimensionality
  • Real-time serving: all algorithms can make decisions in < 1ms — suitable for web traffic

11 · Interpretability Considerations

  • ✅ Thompson Sampling posterior distributions show exactly how confident you are about each price
  • ✅ UCB confidence bounds are intuitive — "we're 95% sure this price converts between 8% and 14%"
  • ✅ Arm selection frequency shows the algorithm's "preference" — easy to explain to stakeholders
  • ✅ Cumulative regret quantifies the cost of learning — "we lost $X while finding the optimal price"
  • ⚠️ Epsilon-greedy is the easiest to explain — "we show the best price 90% of the time and experiment 10%"
  • ⚠️ Thompson Sampling's Bayesian mechanics are harder to explain to non-technical stakeholders

12 · Complete Working Code

import numpy as np
import matplotlib
matplotlib.use('Agg')
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

np.random.seed(42)

# ─── Step 1: Define the pricing problem ───
prices = np.array([49, 59, 69, 79, 89])
# True conversion rates (unknown to the algorithms)
true_conversion = np.array([0.18, 0.14, 0.11, 0.08, 0.05])
# Expected revenue per visitor = price × conversion
true_revenue = prices * true_conversion
optimal_arm = np.argmax(true_revenue)
optimal_revenue = true_revenue[optimal_arm]

print("=== Pricing Problem Setup ===")
print(f"  {'Price':>6} {'Conv Rate':>10} {'Exp Revenue':>12}")
for i in range(len(prices)):
    marker = " ← OPTIMAL" if i == optimal_arm else ""
    print(f"  ${prices[i]:>5} {true_conversion[i]:>10.1%} ${true_revenue[i]:>10.2f}{marker}")

K = len(prices)
T = 10000  # number of customers
n_simulations = 200

# ─── Step 2: Implement bandit algorithms ───
def simulate_reward(arm):
    """Simulate a customer: returns revenue (price if purchased, 0 otherwise)."""
    purchased = np.random.random() < true_conversion[arm]
    return prices[arm] if purchased else 0

def run_epsilon_greedy(T, epsilon=0.1, decay=True):
    Q = np.zeros(K)
    N = np.zeros(K)
    rewards = np.zeros(T)
    arms_chosen = np.zeros(T, dtype=int)

    for t in range(T):
        eps = epsilon / (1 + t * 0.001) if decay else epsilon
        if np.random.random() < eps or t < K:
            arm = t % K if t < K else np.random.randint(K)
        else:
            arm = np.argmax(Q)

        reward = simulate_reward(arm)
        N[arm] += 1
        Q[arm] += (reward - Q[arm]) / N[arm]
        rewards[t] = reward
        arms_chosen[t] = arm

    return rewards, arms_chosen, Q

def run_ucb1(T, c=2.0):
    Q = np.zeros(K)
    N = np.zeros(K)
    rewards = np.zeros(T)
    arms_chosen = np.zeros(T, dtype=int)

    for t in range(T):
        if t < K:
            arm = t
        else:
            ucb_values = Q + c * np.sqrt(np.log(t) / (N + 1e-8))
            arm = np.argmax(ucb_values)

        reward = simulate_reward(arm)
        N[arm] += 1
        Q[arm] += (reward - Q[arm]) / N[arm]
        rewards[t] = reward
        arms_chosen[t] = arm

    return rewards, arms_chosen, Q

def run_thompson_sampling(T):
    alpha = np.ones(K)
    beta_param = np.ones(K)
    rewards = np.zeros(T)
    arms_chosen = np.zeros(T, dtype=int)

    for t in range(T):
        samples = np.array([
            prices[a] * np.random.beta(alpha[a], beta_param[a])
            for a in range(K)
        ])
        arm = np.argmax(samples)

        reward = simulate_reward(arm)
        purchased = reward > 0
        if purchased:
            alpha[arm] += 1
        else:
            beta_param[arm] += 1

        rewards[t] = reward
        arms_chosen[t] = arm

    return rewards, arms_chosen, alpha / (alpha + beta_param)

def run_random(T):
    rewards = np.zeros(T)
    arms_chosen = np.zeros(T, dtype=int)
    for t in range(T):
        arm = np.random.randint(K)
        rewards[t] = simulate_reward(arm)
        arms_chosen[t] = arm
    return rewards, arms_chosen, None

# ─── Step 3: Run simulations ───
algorithms = {
    'Epsilon-Greedy': run_epsilon_greedy,
    'UCB1': run_ucb1,
    'Thompson Sampling': run_thompson_sampling,
    'Random': run_random
}

all_regrets = {name: np.zeros((n_simulations, T)) for name in algorithms}
all_arms = {name: np.zeros((n_simulations, T), dtype=int) for name in algorithms}

print(f"\n=== Running {n_simulations} simulations × {T} customers ===")
for name, algo in algorithms.items():
    for sim in range(n_simulations):
        np.random.seed(sim * 100 + hash(name) % 1000)
        rewards, arms, _ = algo(T)
        regret = optimal_revenue - rewards
        all_regrets[name][sim] = np.cumsum(regret)
        all_arms[name][sim] = arms
    mean_final_regret = all_regrets[name][:, -1].mean()
    print(f"  {name:<20}: Mean cumulative regret = ${mean_final_regret:.0f}")

# ─── Step 4: Detailed single-run analysis ───
np.random.seed(42)
print(f"\n=== Single Run Analysis (Thompson Sampling) ===")
ts_rewards, ts_arms, ts_estimates = run_thompson_sampling(T)

print(f"  Arm selection frequency:")
for i in range(K):
    count = (ts_arms == i).sum()
    print(f"    ${prices[i]}: {count:>5} times ({count/T:.1%}), "
          f"Est conv: {ts_estimates[i]:.3f}, True: {true_conversion[i]:.3f}")

total_revenue = ts_rewards.sum()
optimal_total = optimal_revenue * T
print(f"\n  Total revenue: ${total_revenue:,.0f}")
print(f"  Optimal revenue: ${optimal_total:,.0f}")
print(f"  Revenue captured: {total_revenue/optimal_total:.1%}")

# ─── Step 5: Visualization ───
fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(14, 12))

# Cumulative regret comparison
ax = axes[0, 0]
colors_alg = {'Epsilon-Greedy': '#f0883e', 'UCB1': '#58a6ff',
              'Thompson Sampling': '#3fb950', 'Random': '#f85149'}
for name in algorithms:
    mean_regret = all_regrets[name].mean(axis=0)
    ax.plot(mean_regret, color=colors_alg[name], linewidth=2, label=name)
ax.set_xlabel('Customers')
ax.set_ylabel('Cumulative Regret ($)')
ax.set_title('Cumulative Regret Comparison')
ax.legend(fontsize=9)
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.2)

# Arm selection over time (Thompson Sampling)
ax = axes[0, 1]
window = 200
for i in range(K):
    freq = np.convolve((ts_arms == i).astype(float),
                       np.ones(window)/window, mode='valid')
    ax.plot(freq, label=f'${prices[i]}', linewidth=1.5)
ax.set_xlabel('Customer')
ax.set_ylabel('Selection Frequency (rolling)')
ax.set_title('Thompson Sampling — Arm Selection Over Time')
ax.legend(fontsize=8)
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.2)

# Final regret distribution
ax = axes[1, 0]
final_regrets = {name: all_regrets[name][:, -1] for name in algorithms}
bp = ax.boxplot([final_regrets[n] for n in algorithms],
                labels=list(algorithms.keys()), patch_artist=True)
for patch, name in zip(bp['boxes'], algorithms):
    patch.set_facecolor(colors_alg[name])
    patch.set_alpha(0.7)
ax.set_ylabel('Final Cumulative Regret ($)')
ax.set_title(f'Regret Distribution ({n_simulations} simulations)')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.2, axis='y')
ax.tick_params(axis='x', rotation=15)

# Revenue per arm
ax = axes[1, 1]
x = np.arange(K)
width = 0.35
ax.bar(x - width/2, true_revenue, width, color='#58a6ff', alpha=0.8, label='True Revenue/Visit')
ts_freq = np.array([(ts_arms == i).sum() / T for i in range(K)])
ax.bar(x + width/2, ts_freq * 100, width, color='#3fb950', alpha=0.8, label='TS Selection % (×100)')
ax.set_xticks(x)
ax.set_xticklabels([f'${p}' for p in prices])
ax.set_ylabel('Value')
ax.set_title('True Revenue vs. Thompson Sampling Allocation')
ax.legend(fontsize=8)
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.2, axis='y')

for a in axes.flat:
    a.set_facecolor('#0d1117')
    a.tick_params(colors='white')
    a.xaxis.label.set_color('white')
    a.yaxis.label.set_color('white')
    a.title.set_color('white')
fig.patch.set_facecolor('#0d1117')
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('project_58_bandits.png', dpi=100, bbox_inches='tight', facecolor='#0d1117')
print(f"\n✅ Bandit analysis saved as project_58_bandits.png")
