Project 25: Predicting Sales With Time — Simple Time Series Decomposition

Time Series Trend Seasonality Residuals Decomposition ACF

Difficulty: Medium  |  Builds on: Projects 5, 20

1 · Hypothetical Problem

  • You're a data analyst at a retail chain — management wants to understand monthly sales patterns over the past 4 years
  • Sales seem to go up overall, spike every December, and dip every February
  • They ask: "Is the growth real or just seasonal? What's the underlying trend? Can we separate the pattern from the noise?"
  • Every model we've built so far (Projects 1–24) treats each row as independent — no notion of time ordering
  • Goal → decompose the sales series into trend, seasonality, and residual components to understand what's really happening

2 · How to Approach It

  1. Recall from Project 5: correlation measures linear relationships — but time series have autocorrelation (a value correlates with its own past)
  2. Recall from Project 20: polynomial features captured nonlinear patterns — time series have their own nonlinear structure (cycles, trends)
  3. A time series = Trend + Seasonality + Residual (additive) or Trend × Seasonality × Residual (multiplicative)
  4. Use statsmodels seasonal_decompose to split the series into these three components
  5. Use the ACF (autocorrelation function) to detect repeating patterns and lag dependencies

3 · How Can We Solve It

  1. Generate 4 years of monthly sales data with a known trend, seasonal pattern, and random noise
  2. Visualize the raw series and identify visual patterns
  3. Apply additive and multiplicative decomposition
  4. Plot ACF to confirm the seasonal period and detect autocorrelation structure
  5. Extract the trend to answer "is the business actually growing?"
  6. Examine residuals — if they look random, the decomposition captured the signal

4 · What Should Do

  • ✅ Plot the raw series first — always look at your data before decomposing
  • ✅ Choose additive vs multiplicative based on whether seasonal swings grow with the level
  • ✅ Set the correct period parameter — monthly data with yearly seasonality → period=12
  • ✅ Check residuals after decomposition — they should look like white noise
  • ✅ Use ACF to confirm the seasonal period before assuming it

5 · What Should NOT Do

  • ❌ Do NOT apply standard regression (Project 2) directly to time series — it ignores temporal dependencies
  • ❌ Do NOT assume additive decomposition by default — if seasonal amplitude grows with the level, use multiplicative
  • ❌ Do NOT confuse trend with seasonality — a December spike is seasonal, not a trend
  • ❌ Do NOT ignore the residuals — large residual patterns mean the decomposition missed something
  • ❌ Do NOT use decomposition alone for forecasting — it describes the past but doesn't predict the future (that's Project 26)

6 · When to Use What

  • 🔹 Additive decomposition → when seasonal fluctuations are roughly constant regardless of the level
  • 🔹 Multiplicative decomposition → when seasonal swings grow proportionally with the trend (e.g., 10% holiday bump)
  • 🔹 ACF plot → to detect the seasonal period and check for autocorrelation
  • 🔹 Moving average → to smooth out noise and reveal the trend
  • 🔹 Standard regression → only if the data has no temporal structure (Project 2)

7 · When NOT to Use

  • 🚫 When data has no time ordering — decomposition is meaningless on cross-sectional data
  • 🚫 When the series is too short — you need at least 2 full seasonal cycles (24 months for monthly data)
  • 🚫 When you need forecasts — decomposition is descriptive, not predictive (use ARIMA, Project 26)
  • 🚫 When the seasonal pattern changes over time — classical decomposition assumes a fixed pattern

8 · How It Only Works — Internal Mechanics

Additive vs Multiplicative Model

  • Additive: Y(t) = Trend(t) + Seasonal(t) + Residual(t)
  • Multiplicative: Y(t) = Trend(t) × Seasonal(t) × Residual(t)
  • If you take the log of a multiplicative series, it becomes additive: log(T × S × R) = log(T) + log(S) + log(R)
  • Quick test: if the seasonal amplitude is proportional to the level → multiplicative

How seasonal_decompose Works

  • Step 1: Estimate the trend using a centered moving average (window = period)
  • Step 2: Remove the trend → detrended = observed - trend (additive) or observed / trend (multiplicative)
  • Step 3: Average the detrended values for each season position (e.g., all Januaries) → seasonal component
  • Step 4: Residual = observed - trend - seasonal (additive) or observed / (trend × seasonal) (multiplicative)

Autocorrelation Function (ACF)

  • ACF measures the correlation between a series and its lagged version
  • ACF at lag k = correlation(Y(t), Y(t-k))
  • If ACF spikes at lag 12 → the series repeats every 12 periods (yearly seasonality in monthly data)
  • A slowly decaying ACF suggests a strong trend — the series is not stationary
  • This connects to Pearson correlation (Project 5) — but now the correlation is with the series' own past

Stationarity — Why It Matters

  • A stationary series has constant mean and variance over time — no trend, no changing seasonality
  • Most statistical models (ARIMA, Project 26) require stationarity
  • Decomposition helps you check: if the trend is flat and residuals are random, the detrended series may be stationary
  • Non-stationary series need differencing or detrending before modeling

9 · Common Pitfalls and Failure Modes

  • ⚠️ Wrong period — setting period=4 on monthly data when the true cycle is 12 months
  • ⚠️ Too short a series — decomposition needs at least 2 full cycles; 1 year of monthly data isn't enough
  • ⚠️ Additive on multiplicative data — residuals will show a fan shape (growing variance)
  • ⚠️ Missing values — seasonal_decompose can't handle NaNs in the middle of the series
  • ⚠️ Confusing decomposition with forecasting — decomposition explains the past; it doesn't predict the future

10 · Scalability Considerations

  • Classical decomposition is O(n) — very fast even on long series
  • ACF computation is O(n × max_lag) — fast for typical business data
  • For very long series (millions of points), consider downsampling or STL decomposition (more robust)
  • STL (Seasonal and Trend decomposition using Loess) handles changing seasonality — classical decomposition doesn't
  • Memory is rarely an issue — time series are typically 1D arrays

11 · Interpretability Considerations

  • ✅ Decomposition is highly interpretable — "sales grow $500/month, spike 20% in December, noise is ±$200"
  • ✅ Trend component directly answers "is the business growing?"
  • ✅ Seasonal component shows exactly when peaks and troughs occur
  • ✅ Residuals reveal anomalies — a large residual means something unusual happened
  • ⚠️ The moving average trend loses data at the edges (first and last period/2 points are NaN)

12 · Complete Working Code

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from statsmodels.tsa.seasonal import seasonal_decompose
from statsmodels.graphics.tsaplots import plot_acf
import matplotlib
matplotlib.use('Agg')
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# ─── Step 1: Generate 4 years of monthly sales data ───
np.random.seed(42)
n_months = 48
dates = pd.date_range(start='2020-01-01', periods=n_months, freq='MS')

# Known components
trend = np.linspace(10000, 18000, n_months)  # steady growth
seasonal_pattern = [0, -500, 200, 400, 300, -200, -400, -300, 100, 500, 800, 2000]  # Dec spike
seasonality = np.tile(seasonal_pattern, n_months // 12)
noise = np.random.normal(0, 300, n_months)

sales = trend + seasonality + noise
ts = pd.Series(sales, index=dates, name='monthly_sales')

print("=== Monthly Sales Time Series ===")
print(f"  Period: {dates[0].strftime('%Y-%m')} to {dates[-1].strftime('%Y-%m')}")
print(f"  Points: {n_months}")
print(f"  Min: ${sales.min():,.0f}, Max: ${sales.max():,.0f}")
print(f"  Mean: ${sales.mean():,.0f}, Std: ${sales.std():,.0f}")

# ─── Step 2: Plot the raw series ───
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 4))
ax.plot(dates, sales, color='#58a6ff', linewidth=1.5, marker='o', markersize=3)
ax.set_title('Raw Monthly Sales')
ax.set_ylabel('Sales ($)')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.2)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('project_25_raw_series.png', dpi=100, bbox_inches='tight')
print("\n✅ Raw series plot saved as project_25_raw_series.png")

# ─── Step 3: Additive decomposition ───
decomp_add = seasonal_decompose(ts, model='additive', period=12)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(4, 1, figsize=(12, 10), sharex=True)
decomp_add.observed.plot(ax=axes[0], color='#58a6ff')
axes[0].set_ylabel('Observed')
axes[0].set_title('Additive Decomposition')

decomp_add.trend.plot(ax=axes[1], color='#f0883e')
axes[1].set_ylabel('Trend')

decomp_add.seasonal.plot(ax=axes[2], color='#3fb950')
axes[2].set_ylabel('Seasonal')

decomp_add.resid.plot(ax=axes[3], color='#f85149', marker='o', markersize=2, linestyle='none')
axes[3].set_ylabel('Residual')

for ax in axes:
    ax.grid(True, alpha=0.2)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('project_25_decomposition.png', dpi=100, bbox_inches='tight')
print("✅ Decomposition plot saved as project_25_decomposition.png")

# ─── Step 4: Inspect extracted components ───
print("\n=== Extracted Seasonal Pattern (monthly offsets) ===")
seasonal_vals = decomp_add.seasonal[:12].values
months = ['Jan', 'Feb', 'Mar', 'Apr', 'May', 'Jun',
          'Jul', 'Aug', 'Sep', 'Oct', 'Nov', 'Dec']
for m, v in zip(months, seasonal_vals):
    bar = "+" * int(max(0, v) / 100) + "-" * int(max(0, -v) / 100)
    print(f"  {m}: {v:>+8.0f}  {bar}")

print(f"\n=== Trend Summary ===")
trend_vals = decomp_add.trend.dropna()
print(f"  Start: ${trend_vals.iloc[0]:,.0f}")
print(f"  End:   ${trend_vals.iloc[-1]:,.0f}")
print(f"  Growth: ${trend_vals.iloc[-1] - trend_vals.iloc[0]:,.0f} over {len(trend_vals)} months")
print(f"  Monthly growth: ~${(trend_vals.iloc[-1] - trend_vals.iloc[0]) / len(trend_vals):,.0f}/month")

# ─── Step 5: Residual analysis ───
resid = decomp_add.resid.dropna()
print(f"\n=== Residual Analysis ===")
print(f"  Mean: {resid.mean():.1f} (should be ~0)")
print(f"  Std:  {resid.std():.1f}")
print(f"  Max absolute: {resid.abs().max():.1f}")

# ─── Step 6: ACF plot — detect autocorrelation ───
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(14, 4))

plot_acf(ts, lags=36, ax=axes[0], color='#58a6ff')
axes[0].set_title('ACF of Original Series')
axes[0].grid(True, alpha=0.2)

plot_acf(resid, lags=24, ax=axes[1], color='#f0883e')
axes[1].set_title('ACF of Residuals (after decomposition)')
axes[1].grid(True, alpha=0.2)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('project_25_acf.png', dpi=100, bbox_inches='tight')
print("✅ ACF plots saved as project_25_acf.png")

# ─── Step 7: Multiplicative decomposition comparison ───
# Create multiplicative data: seasonal effect grows with level
trend_mult = np.linspace(10000, 18000, n_months)
seasonal_mult_pattern = [1.0, 0.95, 1.02, 1.04, 1.03, 0.98, 0.96, 0.97, 1.01, 1.05, 1.08, 1.20]
seasonality_mult = np.tile(seasonal_mult_pattern, n_months // 12)
noise_mult = np.random.normal(1, 0.02, n_months)

sales_mult = trend_mult * seasonality_mult * noise_mult
ts_mult = pd.Series(sales_mult, index=dates, name='sales_multiplicative')

decomp_mult = seasonal_decompose(ts_mult, model='multiplicative', period=12)

print(f"\n=== Multiplicative Decomposition ===")
print(f"  Seasonal factors (multiplicative):")
seasonal_factors = decomp_mult.seasonal[:12].values
for m, v in zip(months, seasonal_factors):
    print(f"    {m}: {v:.3f} ({'↑' if v > 1 else '↓'} {abs(v - 1) * 100:.1f}%)")

# ─── Step 8: Additive vs Multiplicative — visual comparison ───
fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 1, figsize=(12, 6))

axes[0].plot(dates, sales, color='#58a6ff', label='Additive data')
axes[0].set_title('Additive: Seasonal swings are constant')
axes[0].set_ylabel('Sales ($)')
axes[0].legend()
axes[0].grid(True, alpha=0.2)

axes[1].plot(dates, sales_mult, color='#f0883e', label='Multiplicative data')
axes[1].set_title('Multiplicative: Seasonal swings grow with level')
axes[1].set_ylabel('Sales ($)')
axes[1].legend()
axes[1].grid(True, alpha=0.2)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('project_25_add_vs_mult.png', dpi=100, bbox_inches='tight')
print("✅ Additive vs Multiplicative comparison saved as project_25_add_vs_mult.png")

# ─── Step 9: Moving average for trend extraction ───
print(f"\n=== Moving Average Smoothing ===")
for window in [3, 6, 12]:
    ma = ts.rolling(window=window, center=True).mean()
    print(f"  Window={window:>2}: smoothed range = ${ma.min():,.0f} – ${ma.max():,.0f}")

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 4))
ax.plot(dates, sales, color='#c9d1d9', alpha=0.4, label='Raw')
for window, color in [(3, '#f85149'), (6, '#3fb950'), (12, '#58a6ff')]:
    ma = ts.rolling(window=window, center=True).mean()
    ax.plot(dates, ma, color=color, linewidth=2, label=f'MA({window})')
ax.set_title('Moving Average Smoothing')
ax.set_ylabel('Sales ($)')
ax.legend()
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.2)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('project_25_moving_average.png', dpi=100, bbox_inches='tight')
print("✅ Moving average plot saved as project_25_moving_average.png")

13 · Key Takeaway + Bridge to Next Project

Key Takeaway

  • Time series data has structure that cross-sectional models (Projects 1–24) ignore: trend, seasonality, and autocorrelation
  • Decomposition splits a series into Trend + Seasonal + Residual — each component tells a different story
  • Choose additive when seasonal swings are constant; multiplicative when they grow with the level
  • ACF reveals the autocorrelation structure — spikes at regular lags confirm seasonality
  • Decomposition is descriptive, not predictive — it explains the past but doesn't forecast the future
