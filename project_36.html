Project 36: Hierarchical Time Series — Forecasting at Store and Region Level

Hierarchical Forecasting Top-Down Bottom-Up Reconciliation Coherent Forecasts

Difficulty: Hard  |  Builds on: Projects 25, 26

1 · Hypothetical Problem

  • You're a demand planner at a retail chain with 3 regions, each containing 3–4 stores (10 stores total)
  • Finance needs national-level forecasts for budgeting; operations needs store-level forecasts for inventory
  • Problem: if you forecast each store independently (Project 26), the store forecasts don't sum to the regional or national forecast
  • Store 1 + Store 2 + Store 3 ≠ Region North forecast — this creates planning chaos
  • Goal → produce coherent forecasts where store-level predictions sum exactly to region and national totals

2 · How to Approach It

  1. From Project 25: time series decomposition handles single series — but we have a hierarchy
  2. From Project 26: ARIMA forecasts individual series — but doesn't enforce cross-series consistency
  3. Three classical strategies: top-down (forecast national, disaggregate), bottom-up (forecast stores, aggregate), middle-out
  4. Modern approach: forecast all levels independently, then reconcile using optimal combination
  5. Use the summing matrix S to define the hierarchy and enforce coherence

3 · How Can We Solve It

  1. Generate synthetic hierarchical sales data (national → region → store)
  2. Forecast each level independently using exponential smoothing
  3. Implement bottom-up reconciliation (sum store forecasts to get region/national)
  4. Implement top-down reconciliation (distribute national forecast to stores by historical proportions)
  5. Compare reconciliation methods on forecast accuracy at each level

4 · What Should Do

  • ✅ Always check coherence — store forecasts must sum to region, regions must sum to national
  • ✅ Use bottom-up when bottom-level data is reliable and has enough history
  • ✅ Use top-down when bottom-level data is noisy or sparse
  • ✅ Evaluate accuracy at ALL levels — a method good for national may be bad for stores
  • ✅ Visualize the hierarchy to understand the aggregation structure

5 · What Should NOT Do

  • ❌ Do NOT forecast each level independently without reconciliation — incoherent forecasts cause planning conflicts
  • ❌ Do NOT assume top-down is always better — it loses bottom-level patterns
  • ❌ Do NOT ignore intermittent demand at store level — sparse data needs special treatment
  • ❌ Do NOT use the same model for all series — some stores may be seasonal, others not
  • ❌ Do NOT evaluate only at the aggregate level — store-level accuracy matters for operations

6 · When to Use What

  • 🔹 Bottom-up → when bottom-level data is rich and reliable; preserves local patterns
  • 🔹 Top-down → when bottom-level data is noisy/sparse; national trends are more stable
  • 🔹 Middle-out → when mid-level (region) data is the most reliable starting point
  • 🔹 Optimal reconciliation → when you want the best of all levels; uses MinT or WLS
  • 🔹 Independent ARIMA (Project 26) → only when coherence doesn't matter

7 · When NOT to Use

  • 🚫 When you only have one time series — no hierarchy to reconcile
  • 🚫 When the hierarchy is flat (no aggregation structure) — just forecast independently
  • 🚫 When real-time forecasting is needed — reconciliation adds latency
  • 🚫 When bottom-level series are too short for meaningful forecasts — top-down is the only option

8 · How It Only Works — Internal Mechanics

The Summing Matrix S

  • Defines how bottom-level series aggregate to higher levels
  • If stores {1,2,3} belong to Region North: y_North = y_1 + y_2 + y_3
  • S maps bottom-level forecasts to all levels: ŷ_all = S × ŷ_bottom
  • Coherence means: the aggregation constraints hold exactly in the forecasts

Bottom-Up Reconciliation

  • Forecast each bottom-level series (stores) independently
  • Aggregate: region forecast = sum of store forecasts; national = sum of regions
  • Coherent by construction — but bottom-level noise propagates upward

Top-Down Reconciliation

  • Forecast only the top level (national)
  • Disaggregate using historical proportions: ŷ_store_i = p_i × ŷ_national
  • p_i = historical share of store i in national sales
  • Coherent by construction — but assumes proportions are stable over time

Optimal Reconciliation (MinT)

  • Forecast all levels independently (base forecasts)
  • Adjust base forecasts to satisfy coherence constraints while minimizing total forecast error
  • Uses the covariance structure of forecast errors to optimally weight adjustments
  • Produces the best unbiased coherent forecasts under certain conditions

9 · Common Pitfalls and Failure Modes

  • ⚠️ Incoherent forecasts — independent forecasts at different levels won't sum correctly; always reconcile
  • ⚠️ Unstable proportions in top-down — if a new store opens, historical proportions are wrong
  • ⚠️ Noisy bottom-level data — bottom-up amplifies noise from sparse store-level series
  • ⚠️ Ignoring negative forecasts — reconciliation can produce negative values; clip to zero
  • ⚠️ Evaluating only at one level — a method may excel at national but fail at store level

10 · Scalability Considerations

  • Bottom-up: scales linearly with number of bottom-level series — forecast each independently
  • Top-down: O(1) forecasting (one model) + O(n) disaggregation — very fast
  • Optimal reconciliation: requires estimating covariance matrix — O(n²) where n = total series
  • For thousands of stores, use diagonal or shrinkage covariance estimators
  • Libraries like scikit-hts and hierarchicalforecast handle large hierarchies efficiently

11 · Interpretability Considerations

  • ✅ Bottom-up is most interpretable — each store has its own model you can inspect
  • ✅ Top-down proportions are easy to explain — "Store X gets 15% of the national forecast"
  • ⚠️ Optimal reconciliation adjustments are harder to explain — "why did my store forecast change?"
  • ⚠️ Stakeholders at different levels care about different metrics — tailor reporting accordingly

12 · Complete Working Code

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from statsmodels.tsa.holtwinters import ExponentialSmoothing
import matplotlib
matplotlib.use('Agg')
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

np.random.seed(42)

# ─── Step 1: Generate hierarchical sales data ───
# Hierarchy: National → 3 Regions → 10 Stores
n_months = 36
dates = pd.date_range('2021-01-01', periods=n_months, freq='MS')

# Store-level base sales with trend and seasonality
stores = {
    'North_S1': 100, 'North_S2': 80, 'North_S3': 120,
    'Central_S4': 150, 'Central_S5': 90, 'Central_S6': 110, 'Central_S7': 70,
    'South_S8': 130, 'South_S9': 95, 'South_S10': 85
}

regions = {
    'North': ['North_S1', 'North_S2', 'North_S3'],
    'Central': ['Central_S4', 'Central_S5', 'Central_S6', 'Central_S7'],
    'South': ['South_S8', 'South_S9', 'South_S10']
}

store_data = {}
for store, base in stores.items():
    trend = np.linspace(0, base * 0.3, n_months)
    season = base * 0.15 * np.sin(2 * np.pi * np.arange(n_months) / 12)
    noise = np.random.normal(0, base * 0.08, n_months)
    store_data[store] = np.maximum(base + trend + season + noise, 10)

df = pd.DataFrame(store_data, index=dates)

# Aggregate to region and national
for region, store_list in regions.items():
    df[region] = df[store_list].sum(axis=1)
df['National'] = df[list(regions.keys())].sum(axis=1)

print("=== Hierarchical Sales Data ===")
print(f"  Months: {n_months}, Stores: {len(stores)}, Regions: {len(regions)}")
print(f"  National total (last month): {df['National'].iloc[-1]:.0f}")
for region, store_list in regions.items():
    print(f"  {region}: {df[region].iloc[-1]:.0f} = " +
          " + ".join([f"{df[s].iloc[-1]:.0f}" for s in store_list]))

# ─── Step 2: Train/test split ───
train_end = 30
train = df.iloc[:train_end]
test = df.iloc[train_end:]
h = len(test)
print(f"\n  Train: {train_end} months, Test: {h} months")

# ─── Step 3: Base forecasts (independent at each level) ───
def forecast_series(series, h):
    try:
        model = ExponentialSmoothing(series, trend='add', seasonal='add',
                                      seasonal_periods=12).fit(optimized=True)
        return model.forecast(h).values
    except Exception:
        model = ExponentialSmoothing(series, trend='add').fit(optimized=True)
        return model.forecast(h).values

all_series = list(stores.keys()) + list(regions.keys()) + ['National']
base_forecasts = {}
for col in all_series:
    base_forecasts[col] = forecast_series(train[col], h)

print(f"\n=== Base Forecasts (Independent) ===")
# Check coherence of base forecasts
for t in range(h):
    national_base = base_forecasts['National'][t]
    region_sum = sum(base_forecasts[r][t] for r in regions.keys())
    print(f"  Month {t+1}: National forecast={national_base:.0f}, "
          f"Sum of regions={region_sum:.0f}, Gap={national_base - region_sum:.0f}")

# ─── Step 4: Bottom-Up Reconciliation ───
print(f"\n=== Bottom-Up Reconciliation ===")
bu_forecasts = {}
for store in stores.keys():
    bu_forecasts[store] = base_forecasts[store]

for region, store_list in regions.items():
    bu_forecasts[region] = sum(bu_forecasts[s] for s in store_list)
bu_forecasts['National'] = sum(bu_forecasts[r] for r in regions.keys())

# Verify coherence
for t in range(h):
    nat = bu_forecasts['National'][t]
    reg_sum = sum(bu_forecasts[r][t] for r in regions.keys())
    assert abs(nat - reg_sum) < 0.01, "Incoherent!"
print("  ✅ Bottom-up forecasts are coherent (stores sum to regions, regions sum to national)")

# ─── Step 5: Top-Down Reconciliation ───
print(f"\n=== Top-Down Reconciliation ===")
# Historical proportions
total_train = train[list(stores.keys())].sum().sum()
proportions = {}
for store in stores.keys():
    proportions[store] = train[store].sum() / total_train

td_forecasts = {}
for store in stores.keys():
    td_forecasts[store] = proportions[store] * base_forecasts['National']

for region, store_list in regions.items():
    td_forecasts[region] = sum(td_forecasts[s] for s in store_list)
td_forecasts['National'] = base_forecasts['National']

print("  Historical proportions:")
for store, prop in proportions.items():
    print(f"    {store}: {prop:.3f} ({prop:.1%})")
print("  ✅ Top-down forecasts are coherent")

# ─── Step 6: Evaluate all methods ───
print(f"\n=== Forecast Accuracy (MAPE) ===")
def mape(actual, predicted):
    return np.mean(np.abs((actual - predicted) / actual)) * 100

methods = {'Independent': base_forecasts, 'Bottom-Up': bu_forecasts, 'Top-Down': td_forecasts}

print(f"{'Level':<15} {'Independent':>12} {'Bottom-Up':>12} {'Top-Down':>12}")
print("-" * 55)

for level in ['National'] + list(regions.keys()):
    actual = test[level].values
    scores = []
    for method_name, forecasts in methods.items():
        m = mape(actual, forecasts[level])
        scores.append(f"{m:.1f}%")
    print(f"{level:<15} {'  '.join(f'{s:>12}' for s in scores)}")

# Store-level average
for method_name, forecasts in methods.items():
    store_mapes = [mape(test[s].values, forecasts[s]) for s in stores.keys()]
    avg = np.mean(store_mapes)
    print(f"  {method_name} avg store MAPE: {avg:.1f}%")

# ─── Step 7: Visualization ───
fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(14, 12))

# National level comparison
ax = axes[0, 0]
ax.plot(dates[:train_end], train['National'], color='#c9d1d9', linewidth=1.5, label='Train')
ax.plot(dates[train_end:], test['National'], color='white', linewidth=2, label='Actual')
ax.plot(dates[train_end:], bu_forecasts['National'], '--', color='#3fb950', linewidth=1.5, label='Bottom-Up')
ax.plot(dates[train_end:], td_forecasts['National'], '--', color='#f0883e', linewidth=1.5, label='Top-Down')
ax.plot(dates[train_end:], base_forecasts['National'], '--', color='#f85149', linewidth=1.5, label='Independent')
ax.set_title('National Level Forecasts')
ax.legend(fontsize=8)
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.2)

# Region level
ax = axes[0, 1]
for region, color in zip(regions.keys(), ['#58a6ff', '#3fb950', '#f0883e']):
    ax.plot(dates[train_end:], test[region], color=color, linewidth=2, label=f'{region} actual')
    ax.plot(dates[train_end:], bu_forecasts[region], '--', color=color, alpha=0.6, label=f'{region} BU')
ax.set_title('Region Level — Bottom-Up Forecasts')
ax.legend(fontsize=7)
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.2)

# Coherence check
ax = axes[1, 0]
incoherence = []
for t in range(h):
    gap = base_forecasts['National'][t] - sum(base_forecasts[r][t] for r in regions.keys())
    incoherence.append(gap)
ax.bar(range(h), incoherence, color='#f85149', alpha=0.7, label='Independent (incoherent)')
ax.axhline(0, color='white', linewidth=0.5)
ax.bar(range(h), [0]*h, color='#3fb950', alpha=0.7, label='Bottom-Up (coherent)')
ax.set_xlabel('Forecast Month')
ax.set_ylabel('National - Sum(Regions)')
ax.set_title('Coherence Gap: Independent vs Reconciled')
ax.legend(fontsize=8)
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.2)

# Proportions
ax = axes[1, 1]
store_names = list(stores.keys())
props = [proportions[s] for s in store_names]
colors_stores = plt.cm.Set3(np.linspace(0, 1, len(store_names)))
ax.pie(props, labels=store_names, colors=colors_stores, autopct='%1.1f%%', startangle=90)
ax.set_title('Top-Down Proportions (Historical)')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('project_36_hierarchical_ts.png', dpi=100, bbox_inches='tight')
print(f"\n✅ Hierarchical forecasting analysis saved as project_36_hierarchical_ts.png")

13 · Key Takeaway + Bridge to Next Project

Key Takeaway

  • Hierarchical time series require coherent forecasts — store forecasts must sum to region and national
  • Bottom-up preserves local patterns but amplifies noise; top-down is stable but loses local detail
  • Optimal reconciliation (MinT) combines the best of both — forecast independently, then reconcile
  • Always evaluate at ALL levels of the hierarchy — a method good for national may fail at store level
